2026 World Cup · prediction-market microstructure

When a goal hits, which market knows first?

xResidual is a live study of how three real-money markets, Kalshi, Polymarket, and the Betfair exchange, price the 2026 World Cup. The headline question is one the literature names but hasn't measured: cross-venue price discovery, which venue moves first when information arrives, and whether that leadership flips between the quiet pre-match window and the high-information in-play window. Everything runs on a millisecond capture pipeline with an on-chain trade-flow layer, and every prediction is pre-committed and graded in the open.
Built by Prabhat M, Mathematics & Statistics at Purdue (also QuantF1), building toward quantitative research.

Findings so far
The pre-match results are in; the in-play price-discovery result fills in as the tournament is played and the pre-registration grades on Jul 19.
The cross-venue gap is mostly margin, not disagreement. De-vigged, Kalshi and Polymarket title prices agree to ~0.15pp; the visible "5 to 8 cent" gap is the house overround (Kalshi ~5.4% vs Polymarket ~3.0%). The durable difference is cost, not price.
And there is no convergence edge to harvest. Tested out-of-sample, a relative-value convergence trade returned a clean null: 6 trades, −2.6pp, Sharpe −1.95. The "edge" is a cost illusion, exactly what law-of-one-price implies. Reported as a negative result because it is one.
The market is hard to beat wherever it's liquid. Across 238 contracts, the deepest market (the title) agrees with the model to ~0.4pp; where the model disagreed most, an independent bookmaker sided with the market, and the error was a confederation bias in the model, fixed with empirical-Bayes shrinkage (+4.6% out-of-sample RPS, DM p≈0.009).
The infrastructure is real. A 24/7 capture pipeline across three venues plus an on-chain layer reading signed flow from Polymarket's CTF Exchange; validated end-to-end at ~172k events / 6 ms median inter-event spacing.
How it's built
An Elo-plus-squad-value goal model (Skellam goal-difference, Dixon-Coles correction, a format-aware Monte-Carlo sim) serves as an independent reference, not a competitor to the market: it exists to ask where model and market disagree and who is right, checked against a third source before any "edge" is believed. Price discovery is estimated with Hasbrouck information share and Gonzalo-Granger component share on de-vigged mid-prices, separately for the pre-match and in-play windows. Everything is reproducible from public data and covered by 112 unit tests; collection runs 24/7 on an always-on pipeline, so every figure regenerates from a clean clone. The stance is consistent throughout: the market is the subject, not the opponent. Analysis travels because it's analysis; predictions only travel when they're right.
